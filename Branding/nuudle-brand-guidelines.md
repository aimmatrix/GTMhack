# Noodle — Brand Guidelines

*Working brand: **Noodle** (spoken) / **nuudle** (written wordmark + domain). Version 2.1 — single-line script logo.*

---

## 1. Brand at a glance

Noodle is the warm, smart way to reach out. It finds the right people, researches them, and writes an email that actually sounds like you — not a robot.

The logo carries the whole strategy in one stroke. The wordmark is **a single unbroken handwritten line** spelling *nuudle*, in electric violet:

- **One continuous line = connection.** The strand never lifts — it threads through every letter, linking them, the same way the product links you to the right people. It's the noodle and the network in one mark.
- **Handwritten = personal.** A human hand wrote this. That's the promise of the product: outreach in *your* voice, not a generic template.
- **Electric violet = intelligent and modern.** It reads AI-forward and energetic, not grey-corporate.

**Tagline:** Own your outreach.

**What it means:** outreach that sounds like you — not a robot. You own the voice, the angle, and the relationship. The tool does the legwork, but the result is unmistakably yours.

## 2. Name & spelling

| Use | Form | Notes |
|-----|------|-------|
| Spoken / in sentences | **Noodle** | Friendly and readable. Capitalised as a proper noun. |
| Wordmark / logo | **nuudle** | Lowercase, single-line script. The hero asset. |
| Domain / handles | **nuudle.ai** | The "uu" spelling makes the domain and trademark easy to secure. |

The name carries three on-strategy meanings — use them deliberately: **connection** (strands that link), **"use your noodle"** (the idiom for *think it through* — the product does the thinking), and **warmth** (human, not spammy).

---

## 3. Positioning & personality

**Positioning:** The warm, human alternative to cold outreach. Where other tools blast generic templates, Noodle sends a few genuinely personal emails in your own voice.

**Personality (in priority order):**

1. Human — it feels like a person wrote it (the handwritten mark says this out loud).
2. Smart — it researches, finds the angle, and writes well.
3. Energetic — modern, quick, confident; the violet brings the spark.
4. Lightly playful — one wink, never a whole circus.

**The brand is not:** loud, jargon-heavy, salesy, cutesy, or corporate-grey.

---

## 4. Logo & mark

**Primary asset.** The `nuudle` wordmark — a single continuous handwritten line in electric violet. This unbroken stroke is the most important thing about the logo. Protect it.

**Construction rules:**

- **Never break the line.** Don't split, segment, or redraw letters individually. The connection concept dies the moment the stroke lifts.
- **Don't re-letter it** in a different font. The specific hand *is* the logo.
- Keep the loops and the consistent stroke weight intact — don't thin, thicken, or distort them.

**Lockups & icon.** The wordmark is long and horizontal, so for square spaces (app icon, favicon, avatar) **derive a monogram from a single continuous loop** drawn in the same hand — for example a lowercase **n** loop or a small knotted "strand" mark. Keep it one stroke, same weight, same violet. Don't cram the whole word into a square.

**Inverse.** On violet or dark backgrounds, use the wordmark in white or lavender mist. On light backgrounds, use it in electric violet.

**Clear space.** Keep empty space around the wordmark equal to the cap height of the "d" on all sides. Nothing intrudes.

**Minimum size.** Wordmark no smaller than 22px tall so the thin script strokes stay legible. Icon no smaller than 24px.

**Don't:**

- Don't break, recolour mid-word, or add a second colour to the stroke.
- Don't add a drop shadow, gradient, outline, or glow.
- Don't rotate, stretch, condense, or skew.
- Don't place it on a busy photo or a low-contrast / similar-violet background.

## 5. Colour

Electric violet leads; a small set of violet-tinted neutrals supports it. Restraint keeps it credible.

| Role | Name | Hex | Use |
|------|------|-----|-----|
| Primary | Electric violet | `#5E06F9` | Logo, primary buttons, links, key accents |
| Primary (soft) | Violet 400 | `#8B5CF6` | Hover, tints, secondary accents, charts |
| Text | Ink violet | `#17082E` | Body text, headings, high-contrast UI |
| Secondary text | Slate | `#6B6580` | Subtitles, captions, muted labels |
| Surface | Lavender mist | `#F3EFFE` | Cards, section backgrounds, highlights |
| Background | Off-white | `#FBFAFD` | Page background |
| Hairlines | Border | `#E7E1F5` | Dividers, card borders |
| *Optional accent* | *Coral* | *`#FF7A5C`* | *A warm human counterpoint — use ≤5%, for the occasional highlight only* |

**Usage ratio (60 / 30 / 10):** ~60% off-white/white, ~30% ink + neutrals, ~10% electric violet. The optional coral is a garnish on top of that 10%, never a base. The violet is loud on its own — a little goes a long way.

**Accessibility:** Electric violet on off-white passes for large text and UI; for small body text use ink violet. White on electric violet is strong and high-contrast — good for primary buttons.

## 6. Typography

The logo is script; **everything else is a clean sans.** The handwriting stays special precisely because it's used *only* in the logo.

- **Logo:** the single-line handwritten script (locked asset — never reused for other text).
- **Headings & UI:** a clean geometric grotesque — **Geist** or **Inter** (free, web-ready), Medium (500).
- **Body:** the same sans, Regular (400).

**Rules:** two weights maximum; sentence case everywhere (never ALL CAPS); generous body line height (≈1.6); never set headings or body in a script or handwritten font — that space belongs to the logo alone.

---

## 7. Voice & tone

Write the way the product writes: like a thoughtful person, not a marketing department.

**Do:** short, confident sentences; warm and direct; contractions are good; lead with the benefit; use the food/connection metaphor *occasionally* as a wink.

**Don't:** no buzzwords (synergy, leverage, game-changing); no exclamation spam or emoji in product UI; don't over-explain — if a sentence can be cut, cut it.

**Taglines (in priority order):**

- ***Own your outreach.*** (primary — the brand line)
- *Outreach that sounds like you — not a robot.* (the explainer)
- *Find your people. Sound like yourself.*
- *Use your noodle.* (the wink — short form, app store, swag)
- *Cold outreach, served warm.*

**Voice example — an in-brand outreach email:**

> Subject: quick one on your logistics SaaS portfolio
>
> Hi Sam,
>
> Saw you backed two freight-tech teams this year — we're building in the same space and cutting onboarding time by 40%. Thought it might be worth a quick chat before we open our round.
>
> Free for 15 minutes next week?
>
> Cheers,
> Ammad

## 8. Product feature naming

Name the product's parts in-world so the app feels cohesive and on-brand:

| Generic name | Branded name | Why |
|--------------|-------------|-----|
| Voice Profile | **Secret Sauce** | Sells the differentiator in two words — the flavour that makes emails taste like you. |
| A batch / run of matches | **a bowl** | "You've got a fresh bowl of 12 matches." |
| The search step | **finding your people** | Plain, warm, human. |

Lowercase in running UI ("your secret sauce"); capitalised as a menu label.

---

## 9. Application notes

- **Loading state** — animate the wordmark **drawing itself** as one continuous stroke, left to right, while matches stream in. The single-line logo is built for this; it turns a wait into a signature brand moment.
- **App icon** — a single-loop monogram (see §4), electric violet on off-white, or white on violet.
- **Empty state** — honest and warm: "No strong matches this time — try widening the search." Never pad results.
- **Buttons** — electric-violet fill with white text for the primary action; one primary action per screen; outline-only for secondary.
- **Email compliance footer** — sender identity + opt-out line, slate, small, always present on send.
